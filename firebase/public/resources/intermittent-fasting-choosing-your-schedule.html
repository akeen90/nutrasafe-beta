Intermittent Fasting: Choosing Your Schedule

Compare popular fasting windows, align them with your training, and avoid common mistakes when you start intermittent fasting.

Compare fasting schedules

How to choose the right window

  • Match your chronotype: Morning trainers often do better with an earlier window (10am–6pm).
  • Consider work and family: Pick a window you can keep 5+ days a week; consistency matters more than intensity.
  • Health status: If you have diabetes, low BMI, or are pregnant, speak to a clinician before fasting.
  • Step down gradually: Start with 12:12 for a week, then 14:10, then 16:8.

Fueling workouts while fasting

Training fasted is fine for low–moderate sessions. For strength or high-intensity work:

  • Place the eating window around training when possible (e.g., 11am lift, 10–6 window).
  • Prioritise protein (0.3–0.4 g/kg) and 20–40g carbs post-workout to support recovery.
  • Use electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) during longer fasts to reduce headaches and lightheadedness.

Refeed and hydration tips

  • Break the fast with lean protein, fibre, and healthy fats—avoid a sugar spike.
  • Hydrate with 2–3 litres of water daily; add electrolytes on 18:6 or longer windows.
  • Include micronutrient-dense foods: oily fish for omega-3, leafy greens for magnesium, eggs for B12 and choline.
  • Sleep impacts ghrelin/leptin—poor sleep makes fasting harder. Aim for 7–9 hours.

Mistakes to avoid

  • Under-eating protein (target 1.6–2.2 g/kg while cutting).
  • Using coffee only—low electrolytes drive headaches and “keto flu” feelings.
  • Making the window too short too soon; it leads to rebounds and late-night binges.
  • Skipping resistance training—muscle is protective during fat loss.

FAQ

Can I drink coffee while fasting?

Yes—black coffee or plain tea is fine. Add electrolytes to water, not sugar.

Do women need a different approach?

Many do better with 14:10 or 16:8 and avoiding long fasts around the late luteal phase. Listen to energy, sleep, and cycle regularity.
