Why tracking micronutrients matters

Micronutrients drive energy, immunity, and recovery. Subtle shortfalls in vitamin D, B12, iron, or magnesium can cause fatigue and poor performance long before blood tests show a deficiency.

  • Vitamin D: immunity, mood, bone health.
  • Iron: oxygen transport and endurance.
  • B12 & Folate: red blood cell formation and cognition.
  • Magnesium: muscle function, sleep quality, and stress response.

Daily targets for key vitamins and minerals

Start with evidence-based reference ranges. NutraSafe uses UK guidance and flags low-intake trends.

  • Vitamin D: 10–25 µg (400–1000 IU) depending on exposure.
  • Iron: 8–18 mg (women typically need more during reproductive years).
  • Calcium: ~700–1000 mg for bone density.
  • Magnesium: 270–400 mg to support sleep and muscle function.
  • Omega-3: Aim for 250–500 mg EPA/DHA from oily fish or supplements.

How to log micronutrients accurately

1) Use verified entries first

Choose NutraSafe’s verified foods to avoid crowd-sourced errors. These entries include full vitamin and mineral panels.

2) Log portion size precisely

Weigh whole foods in grams. For packaged items, scan the barcode to capture label accuracy. NutraSafe automatically scales micronutrients to your portion.

3) Track supplements separately

Log supplements so daily totals include both food and capsules. This prevents accidental overdosing (e.g., vitamin A, zinc).

4) Review weekly trends

Use the weekly dashboard to spot consistent gaps—common offenders are vitamin D in winter and magnesium in high-exercise blocks.

Tips to optimise absorption

  • Pair iron with vitamin C (citrus, peppers) and avoid tea/coffee within an hour.
  • Take vitamin D with a meal containing fat for better uptake.
  • Balance calcium and iron across meals to reduce competition.
  • Spread magnesium across the day if higher doses upset your stomach.

Common tracking mistakes to avoid

  • Relying on generic entries with missing micronutrients.
  • Logging “1 serving” without adjusting the gram weight.
  • Ignoring supplements—this can push zinc or vitamin A above safe upper limits.
  • Not reviewing 7-day averages; single-day highs or lows can mislead.

FAQ

Do I need blood tests?

Use tracking to maintain consistent intake; if you experience symptoms or follow a restrictive diet, ask your GP for blood work (vitamin D, ferritin, B12).
