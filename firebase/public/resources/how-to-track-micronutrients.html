How to Track Micronutrients and Vitamins

Nutrition Guide • UK • Updated January 2025
In this guide: Why tracking matters Daily targets for key vitamins and minerals How to log micronutrients accurately Food sources to prioritise Tracking mistakes to avoid FAQ

Why tracking matters

Micronutrients power energy, mood, immunity, and recovery. Small gaps in vitamin D, B12, iron, or magnesium can cause fatigue long before blood tests show a deficiency. Tracking highlights patterns so you can fix them early.

Daily targets for key vitamins and minerals

  • Vitamin D: 10–25 µg (400–1000 IU) depending on sun exposure.
  • Iron: 8–18 mg (women usually need more during reproductive years).
  • Calcium: ~700–1000 mg for bone health.
  • Magnesium: 270–400 mg to support sleep and muscle function.
  • Omega-3 (EPA/DHA): 250–500 mg from oily fish or supplements.

NutraSafe shows UK-aligned targets and a rolling 7-day average so a single low day does not skew the picture.

How to log micronutrients accurately

1) Use verified entries first

Choose NutraSafe verified foods to avoid crowd-sourced errors. These include full vitamin and mineral panels.

2) Log precise portions

Weigh whole foods in grams. For packaged items, scan the barcode—NutraSafe scales micronutrients to your portion automatically.

3) Track supplements separately

Include supplements so totals reflect both food and capsules. This prevents overdoing zinc or vitamin A.

4) Review weekly trends

Use the weekly dashboard to spot consistent gaps—vitamin D in winter and magnesium during heavy training are common.

Food sources to prioritise

  • Vitamin D: oily fish, fortified milks, eggs (supplement in winter).
  • Iron: lean red meat, lentils, spinach + vitamin C foods for absorption.
  • B12: eggs, dairy, fish; consider supplements if plant-based.
  • Magnesium: pumpkin seeds, almonds, leafy greens, whole grains.
  • Calcium: dairy, fortified plant milks, tofu, sesame/almonds.

Tracking mistakes to avoid

  • Logging “1 serving” without adjusting the gram weight.
  • Relying on generic entries missing micronutrient data.
  • Ignoring supplements in your totals.
  • Not checking 7-day averages; single days can mislead.

FAQ

Do I need blood tests?

Tracking keeps intake consistent. If you have symptoms, follow a restrictive diet, or are pregnant, ask your GP for tests (vitamin D, ferritin, B12).

Can I export my data?

Yes—NutraSafe lets you export weekly micronutrient reports to share with a nutritionist or clinician.
